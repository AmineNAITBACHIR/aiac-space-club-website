The birth of life

Planets outnumber the stars. They are hostile to life as we know. But what do we know about life? We’ve only met one kind so far, earth life. We shouldn’t forget that human eyes see only a sliver of the light that shines in the cosmos. But science gives us the power to see what our senses cannot. Throw an infrared sensor across the darkness in the space and Rogue planet will be visible, it’s a world without a sun. Our galaxy has billions of them. How many ways of life are then in our galaxy? The cosmos on the grandest scale we know -what we call the observable universe- is a network of a hundred billion galaxies. And it’s just our cosmic horizon. And what’s beyond it? We don’t know. It’s because there hasn’t been enough time in the 13,8 billion history of the universe for their light to have reached us. Most of the cosmologists believe that the observable universe is just one tiny bubble in an infinite ocean of other universes. Worlds without end. The universe is 13,8 thousand million years old. How could we humans who live rarely more than a century hope to grasp the vast expanse of time that is the history of the cosmos? Let’s go back as far as we can to the very first moment of the universe: The big bang. Our entire universe emerged from a point smaller than a single atom. Space itself exploded in a cosmic fire launching the expansion of the universe and giving birth to all the energy and all the matter we know today. As it expanded, the universe cooled. There was darkness for about 200 million years. Gravity was pulling together clumps of gas and heating them until the first stars burst into light. The first stars coalesced into the first small galaxies. These galaxies merged to form larger ones including our milky way which formed about 11 billion years ago. A supernova is a blazing death of a giant star. Stars die and are born in a stellar nursery. They condense like raindrops from giant clouds of gas and dust. They get so hot that the nuclei of the atoms fuse together deep within them to make the oxygen we breathe, the carbon in our muscles, the calcium in our bones, the iron in our blood, all of it was cooked in the fiery hearts of long-vanished stars. You, me, everyone; we are made of star stuff. Earth was also formed from a disk of gas and dust orbiting the sun. As the earth cooled, seas began to form. The first aspects of life were established.
